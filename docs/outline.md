## 1. Organizational Overview & HAAG Literacy Training

### 1.1 Purpose of HAAG Literacy

Every member who may interact with an external stakeholder must be able to represent HAAG accurately, professionally, and consistently. HAAG literacy means understanding what the organization is, what it can do, what it cannot promise, and how it operates so that no stakeholder receives a mismatched expectation and there is clear communication.

### 1.2 Core HAAG Knowledge Areas

All members must be trained across five knowledge domains before engaging with stakeholders:

**Domain 1 — Mission & Identity**
- What HAAG is and what problem it exists to solve
- The dual-community model: CS students seeking research experience + researchers seeking technical support
- What makes HAAG different and the benefits it provides (i.e. selling points)
- What HAAG can and cannot commit to (scope, timelines, etc.)

**Domain 2 — Project Lifecycle**
- How projects enter the pipeline  
- Typical project duration and milestone structure
- What deliverables HAAG produces vs. what stakeholders are responsible for
- How changes are handled

**Domain 3 — Member Capabilities & Limitations**
- General skill categories available (e.g., data analysis, software development, ML/AI, visualization, research support)
- Historical fields/areas of HAAG research conducted
- How to describe what a team can attempt vs. guarantee

**Domain 4 — Communication & Engagement Norms**
- How HAAG communicates internally vs. externally
- Recording of meeting notes throughout collaboration lifecycle
- Escalation paths when something goes wrong

**Domain 5 — Organizational Structure**
- Who holds leadership roles and what they decide
- How decisions get made (consensus, leadership call, etc.)
- What a point person is and why the role exists
- How to create opportunities as a point person

### 1.3 Training Format

| Phase | Outcome |
|-------|---------|
| Phase 1: Background | Presented written onboarding document covering the 5 domains + FAQ | Member reads core HAAG materials |
| Phase 2: Orientation Session | Live session with leadership |   Member hears real examples, asks questions |
| Phase 3: Shadowing | Member shadows an active point person on a live stakeholder interaction or reviews a recording of interaction| Observes real communication in context |

### 1.4 Ongoing Literacy (awarness of duties/HAAG)

- Members who have sat through or reviewed stakeholder interactions can be reviewed by leadership member to receive a **"Stakeholder-Ready" designation** tracked in the internal member registry.
- Members who have been inactive for 6+ months must complete a re-orientation and are removed from stakeholder-ready designation.
- Onboarding document/FAQ are updated yearly as need-be with any organizational changes

## 2. Point Person Identification & Matching

### 2.1 What Is a Point Person?

A HAAG **Point Person** is the designated primary contact between HAAG and an external stakeholder for a specific project or engagement. They are the relationship owner, the communication hub, and the accountability holder on HAAG's side. Each engagement also has a **Stakeholder Point Person**  the designated primary contact on the external side.

Defining both clearly and early is the single most important step in preventing communication breakdown.

### 2.2 HAAG Point Person Selection Criteria

The HAAG Point Person should be selected based on:

 1. If they have stakeholder ready designation
 2. They can commit appropriate time for meetings as need be for the length of engagement
 3. They are comfortable speaking in a leadership position and can always make sure to orient conversations back towards the initial goal of the collaboration

### 2.4 Stakeholder Point Person Identification

During the first stakeholder meeting, HAAG must confirm who the Stakeholder Point Person is. This CANNOT be assumed. Example:

> *"To make sure we're communicating efficiently, we'd like to confirm: who on your end will be the primary contact for this project? This is the person our point person will coordinate with directly for updates, questions, and decisions."*

Document the following for the Stakeholder Point Person:

- Full name and title
- Organization and department
- Preferred communication channel (email, Slack, etc.)
- Availability windows
- Backup contact if they are unavailable

## 3. Consistency Measurement & Transfer Triggers

### 3.1 Why This Matters

Inconsistency in a point person regardless of whether in communication frequency, accuracy of information shared, or quality of follow-through erodes stakeholder trust within HAAG and can ruin collaboration opportunities. This section defines how to detect inconsistency and how to determine when a transfer is needed.

### 3.2 Consistency Metrics

Track the following on a per-point-person basis (based on assumptions):

**Communication Consistency**
- Are updates delivered on the agreed schedule?
- Are stakeholder messages responded to within the agreed window/respectable time frame ?
- Is the tone and content of messages aligned with HAAG norms?

**Engagement Quality**
- Does the point person attend all scheduled check-ins?
- Are meeting notes or summaries being logged after stakeholder interactions?
- Is the point person documenting action items and tracking their resolution?

**Stakeholder Satisfaction Signal**
- Periodic check-in: ask the stakeholder directly: "How is communication going?", "Have there been any areas of friction?", "Is there anything concerning you?"
- Watch for signs of frustration: repeated re-asks, escalations, requests to speak with someone else.

### 3.4 Transfer Triggers

A transfer should be considered/may be required if any of the following conditions are met:

- Point person misses two or more stakeholder check-ins without prior notice
- Stakeholder reports a miscommunication that caused project work to be redone
- Point person makes a scope or timeline commitment without leadership sign-off
- Point person is unresponsive to internal HAAG communications  
- Relationship tension has developed between the point person and the stakeholder
- Point person self-reports feeling unable to continue effectively

**Potential Path**
1. Leadership identifies or receives a concerning event to trigger an investigation.
2. A one-on-one conversation is held with the point person to understand context.
3. Leadership determines: remediation plan vs. transfer.
4. If transfer is needed: initiate Handoff Protocol

## 4. Handoff Protocols

### 4.1 Principles of a Good Handoff

Handoffs are necessary and natural part of organizations. Our goal here is that the stakeholder ends the handoff process feeling *more* confident in HAAG, not less.

### 4.2 Handoff Preparation Checklist

Before the incoming point person takes over, the outgoing point person must complete the following:

**Documentation Package**
1. Project summary: current status, completed milestones, remaining work
2. Stakeholder profile: name, role, communication preferences, decision-making authority, personality notes
3. Communication history: summary of key exchanges, any outstanding commitments, as well as all of the meeting notes
4. Open action items: what has been promised, to whom, and by when
5. Important Information: anything the incoming point person must know to avoid a misstep
6. Access transfer: any shared documents, channels, or tools the stakeholder uses to communicate

**Knowledge Transfer Session**
- A 30–60 minute meeting between outgoing and incoming point persons is required for all handoffs.

### 4.3 Stakeholder Communication

The stakeholder must be informed of the handoff before it happens.

1. Leadership sends the notification not the outgoing point person alone.
2. Frame it positively if possible and emphasize continuity of the collaboration, not focusing on the departure.
3. Introduce the incoming point person by name and a sentence about their background.
4. Abide by a transition overlap window where both point persons should be cc'd on communications for at least one full week.
5. Invite the stakeholder to ask questions about the change if need be.

### 4.5 Post-Handoff Review

Two weeks after a handoff is completed, leadership should:
- Check in with the incoming point person on how the transition felt
- Send a brief, informal pulse to the stakeholder to confirm they feel supported
- Document any lessons learned in the handoff log for future improvement

## 5. Point Person Training 

### 5.1 Why This Matters

A  point person who only executes exactly what is asked will miss opportunities to deepen HAAG's impact and the organization's relationship with that stakeholder. This section trains point persons in both directions: listening well and thinking expansively.

### 5.2 Needs Identification Training

Train point persons to use a structured conversation built around these questions:

**Context**
- What is your research trying to answer or accomplish?
- Who is the audience for this work?
- What have you already tried or built toward this goal?
- What would success look like in 6 months? In a year?

**Constraints**
- What is your timeline, and is there a hard deadline?
- Do you have data already, or is data collection part of the work?
- How much of your own time can you commit to this collaboration?

### 5.3 Opportunity Expansion 

- A point person needs to have it written down clearly what it is they need from the stakeholder, this should be identified and whenever presented with an opportunity from the stakeholder they need to think how can this be tied back to what we need and or expanded upon to further encompass HAAG

**Framing Expansion Conversations**

Expansion opportunities should always be framed as questions, not proposals:
- *"We noticed X while working on this, would it be useful if we also looked at Y?"*
- *"Would it be helpful to document this in a way that could be shared with other teams?"*
- *"Is this a one-time need, or do you foresee similar projects in the future?"*
